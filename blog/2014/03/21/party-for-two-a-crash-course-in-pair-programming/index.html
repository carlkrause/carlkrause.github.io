Party for Two: A Crash Course in Pair Programming

Mar 21st, 2014

Pair programming is a huge part of the Dev Bootcamp curriculum and I’m just getting my feet wet. In past jobs I’ve worked very closely with coworkers on projects, often sitting at a computer with someone as one person comes up with copy or e-mail text and the other types and offers instant feedback. It forces both people to focus on quality and getting it done quickly.

Pairing

This week I was a little behind the curve with pairing: my first two weeks of Phase 0 were particularly busy at work and I missed out on a few pairing opportunities. Nonetheless, when I started pairing this week I really enjoyed it.

First of all, it was so emotionally helpful to put a face to my peers’ names and see that they were struggling with the same concepts I was. My feeling the first week was that I was so far behind and would struggle to catch up, but just working through problems and chatting was immensely helpful in making me realize that if I A) did all the work and B) continued to have enthusiasm for my learning I was going to meet my goals.

I have found that pairs have very different ways of thinking and working. The two people I paired with this week were polar opposites. I’m always a back-of-the-napkin outliner who then charges into the task when I have a very loose framework. It’s not a bad attribute because I can brainstorm different ways of doing things, but ultimately a pair who says “OK, let’s focus on one approach, give it depth, and get it done” complements my working style. That’s not to say pairing with a person just like me is bad: I ended up learning a lot when pairing when someone much like myself, though our productivity wasn’t as high.

I’m learning my style and also ways to manage myself in a pair. I think I need more practice before I can understand how best to conduct myself in pair programming, but the two I paired with this week were really smart and enthused and I got so much out of each experience.

Feedback

I read and rated at least 20 other students’ feedback this week. It’s definitely helped me write better feedback for my pairs. It’s something I enjoy doing because I felt like we built trust during the sessions and I know that giving constructive feedback will help me receive and be receptive to constructive feedback.

The most difficult thing when writing feedback is how to provide actionable suggestions. When my knowledge of things isn’t 100% I feel like I can’t properly say whether or not someone needs more work on something.

Overall, I really enjoyed my pairing this week: it’s already proving a great productivity tool and much more fun than staring at a screen alone.
